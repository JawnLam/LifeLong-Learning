# Ab Urbe Condita, Books 1–5 — Livy

## What this source is

Books 1–5 of Livy's monumental history of Rome, covering the period from the founding (753 BC) through the Gallic sack of Rome (~390 BC). The Penguin Classics edition (translated by Aubrey de Sélincourt, titled *The Early History of Rome*) is the standard accessible English version.

This is a primary source in two senses: (1) it is itself an ancient text and (2) it is the canonical Latin narrative through which the Roman story of early Rome has reached us. But it is *not* a primary source for the events it describes — Livy is writing in the 20s BC about events 700+ years earlier, working from earlier annalistic tradition and Greek-influenced historiography. He should be read accordingly.

## Why it matters here

For Phase 1, Livy provides:

- The narrative texture the analytical sources lack
- The Roman *self-understanding* of their own origins (which is itself a historical fact, distinct from what physically happened)
- A literarily superb account of foundational episodes — Lucretia, Brutus, the early Republic, Camillus
- The starting point for understanding how Roman political rhetoric (especially in the Late Republic) deploys early-Roman exempla

## Approach notes

- **Pace:** Read the five books as literature first, then return to specific episodes when working on related Units
- **Depth:** Read with Beard's interpretive caveats in mind. The events as Livy tells them are not the events as they happened.
- **What to watch for:** Note which episodes feel literarily over-shaped — these are usually the legendary ones. Cattle counts and military details tend to be more reliable than dramatic set-pieces.
- **Pairing:** Read after Beard's relevant chapter (to have the analytical frame); pair with Polybius Book 6 (for the institutional frame Livy assumes).
