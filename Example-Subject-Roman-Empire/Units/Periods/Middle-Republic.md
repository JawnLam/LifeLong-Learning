# Middle Republic (264 – 146 BC)

## Character

The period of Rome's transformation from an Italian regional power into the dominant force in the Mediterranean. Bounded at the start by the outbreak of the First Punic War (264 BC) and at the end by the destruction of both Carthage and Corinth (146 BC) — the famous "year of two annihilations" that left Rome without a peer rival in the Mediterranean.

This is the Rome Polybius lived in and wrote about. Institutionally stable, militarily formidable, expanding through both deliberate conquest and reactive intervention. It is the Rome of the famous Republican virtues — and it is the Rome whose very success creates the structural pressures that will tear the Republic apart in the next century.

## Key Institutions in Force

- **Mature Republican constitution** — consuls, praetors, censors, quaestors, aediles, tribunes; the cursus honorum as Polybius describes it
- **Senate dominance** — the Senate now effectively controls foreign policy, finance, and provincial administration, though formal sovereignty remains with the people
- **Provincial system emerging** — Sicily (the first province, 241 BC), Sardinia/Corsica, the Spains, Macedonia, Africa — administered by ex-praetors and ex-consuls with prorogued imperium
- **Standing client networks** — Roman aristocrats accumulate clienteles abroad, especially in newly-conquered or allied territories
- **The Italian alliance system** — Rome's primary military strength is the *socii*, allied Italian states providing troops on Roman terms

## Major Events Within

- First Punic War (264–241 BC) — Rome contests and wins Sicily
- [[Second-Punic-War]] (218–201 BC) — Hannibal in Italy; Cannae (216); Scipio Africanus; Zama (202)
- Macedonian Wars (215–148 BC) — successive interventions in the Hellenistic east; Pydna (168 BC) ends Antigonid Macedon
- Syrian War (192–188 BC) — defeat of the Seleucid king Antiochus III
- Third Macedonian War / Battle of Pydna (168 BC) — Polybius taken to Rome as a hostage in the aftermath
- Third Punic War (149–146 BC) — destruction of Carthage
- Achaean War (146 BC) — destruction of Corinth, formal Roman control of Greece

## Inflection Out

146 BC, the "year of two annihilations." Carthage destroyed, Corinth destroyed. Rome has no peer rival left. The internal pressures of empire — what to do with conquered land, how to provide for veteran soldiers, how to manage Italian allies who fought Rome's wars without receiving Roman citizenship — will explode in 133 BC when Tiberius Gracchus is elected tribune. The Late Republic begins.

## Concepts Operative in This Period

- [[Mixed-Constitution]] (Polybius's analysis describes *this* Rome)
- [[Cursus-Honorum]] (operating as designed for most of the period)
- [[Imperium]] (extending in unprecedented ways through prorogation for provincial commands)
- [[Mos-Maiorum]] (still functioning as a binding constraint, though showing strain at the edges)

## Thinkers / Sources for This Period

- [[Polybius]] — the period's great analyst; he lived through its second half
- [[Livy]] — Books 21–45 cover the Second Punic War and the Macedonian / Syrian wars (very rich)
- Plutarch, *Lives* — Marcellus, Fabius Maximus, Cato the Elder, the Gracchi (late-Middle-Republic forerunners), Aemilius Paullus
- Modern: Beard, *SPQR* (chapters on this period); Goldsworthy, *The Punic Wars*; Eckstein, *Mediterranean Anarchy, Interstate War, and the Rise of Rome*
