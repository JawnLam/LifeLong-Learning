# Second Punic War (218 – 201 BC)

## What Happened

The seventeen-year war between Rome and Carthage in which Hannibal Barca, leading a Carthaginian army with war elephants, crossed the Alps in 218 BC, invaded Italy, and inflicted on Rome a series of catastrophic defeats — most spectacularly Cannae (216 BC), where some 50,000 Romans died in a single day. Hannibal then occupied parts of Italy for fifteen years without ever being decisively defeated there.

Rome did not surrender. Instead it adapted: avoiding pitched battles in Italy (the *Fabian strategy* under Quintus Fabius Maximus *Cunctator*), bleeding Hannibal's army through attrition, opening new fronts in Spain (under Publius Cornelius Scipio, later "Africanus"), and finally invading Africa itself. Scipio drew Hannibal back to defend Carthage, then defeated him at the Battle of Zama in 202 BC. The war ended in 201 BC with crushing terms imposed on Carthage: indemnity, loss of overseas territories, military restrictions, and effective subordination to Rome.

## Why It Mattered

- **Rome becomes a Mediterranean power.** Before the war, Rome was the dominant Italian state, with one overseas province (Sicily). After it, Rome controls Sicily, Sardinia, Corsica, and a significant portion of the Iberian peninsula — the first real overseas empire.
- **Institutional stress test.** The Republic absorbed Cannae — a defeat that would have ended most states — and reorganized to fight on. Polybius will use this as Exhibit A of the mixed constitution's resilience.
- **The rise of the great commander.** Scipio Africanus's career — long prorogued imperium, command in Spain at unprecedented youth, semi-independent operations in Africa — prefigures the late-Republican generalissimos. The Republic worked, but the war planted seeds of what would later destroy it.
- **The Hannibalic memory.** "Hannibal at the gates" becomes a permanent fixture of Roman political vocabulary. The trauma of the Italian invasion shapes Roman war-fighting psychology for generations.

## Causes

- **Underlying:** The Carthaginian project to rebuild after the First Punic War (241 BC) — especially Hamilcar Barca's and his sons' expansion in Spain. Roman anxiety about Carthaginian recovery; competing spheres of influence.
- **Trigger:** Hannibal's siege of Saguntum (a Roman ally in Spain) in 219 BC. Rome demanded his recall; Carthage refused. Rome declared war.
- **Strategic surprise:** Hannibal's invasion route through the Alps was not anticipated by Rome, which expected the war to be fought in Spain and Africa, not Italy.

## Sources / Disputes

- **Polybius, *Histories* Books 3, 7–15** — the major analytical source; Polybius's patron Scipio Aemilianus was Scipio Africanus's adopted grandson, giving Polybius access to family tradition
- **Livy, *Ab Urbe Condita* Books 21–30** — the great Latin narrative; literarily superb, historically rich, somewhat dramatized
- **Modern:** Goldsworthy, *The Punic Wars*; Lazenby, *Hannibal's War*

The two main sources agree on the broad outline but differ in detail (e.g., the exact pass over the Alps; the precise course of certain battles). Both are pro-Roman in framing, though Polybius is more analytically critical.

## Concepts This Instantiates

- [[Imperium]] in its extended provincial form — Scipio's command in Spain is one of the first long-duration prorogations of military authority outside Italy
- [[Mixed-Constitution]] — Polybius's argument that Rome's system worked partly *because* it could absorb a defeat like Cannae and continue fighting
- The early shape of [[Provincial-Government]] as Rome learns how to administer territories that aren't allied Italian states but are subjects
