# Mos Maiorum

## One-Sentence Statement

*To be developed in first TEACH session.*

The unwritten body of ancestral custom that, for the Romans, did the work that a written constitution does for modern states — defining who could hold what office, how Romans treated each other, and what counted as legitimate political action.

## Precursor / Problem

Rome had no written constitution. Yet for centuries the Roman political system held together through enormous expansion, internal class struggle, and military strain. What was holding it together?

## Full Exposition

*To be developed in study.*

The phrase *mos maiorum* literally means "the way of the ancestors" or "the custom of the elders." It functioned as:

- The basis of legal precedent (alongside formal statute and praetorian edict)
- The legitimating frame for political action ("this is how our ancestors did it")
- A weapon in political argument — appeal to the *mos maiorum* could shut down innovation
- A genuine constraint on behavior the Romans took with deep seriousness

## Analogies

- The British "unwritten constitution"
- Common-law tradition
- Family practice / household norms that govern without being written down

## Counter-Examples / Boundaries

- Not the same as written law (*lex*)
- Not unchanging — the *mos maiorum* clearly evolved, even as the Romans rhetorically treated it as fixed
- Eventually became insufficient: by the Late Republic, appeals to *mos maiorum* were increasingly cynical, and the system it supported collapsed

## Applications

- [[Polybius]] writes about Roman customs and institutions as a stable system; *mos maiorum* is the soil that system grew in
- [[Cursus-Honorum]] is partly defined by custom, partly by law — both layers operate
- Late Republic dysfunction can be read as the collapse of shared belief in *mos maiorum*
