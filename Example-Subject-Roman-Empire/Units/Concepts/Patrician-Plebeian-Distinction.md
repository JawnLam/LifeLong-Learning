# Patrician–Plebeian Distinction

## One-Sentence Statement

The hereditary division of the Roman citizen body into two orders — a small original aristocracy (patricians) and everyone else (plebeians) — whose centuries-long struggle (the *Conflict of the Orders*) gradually opened political offices to plebeians and shaped the institutional architecture of the Republic.

## Precursor / Problem

In the early Republic, only patricians could hold the major magistracies. But the plebeians provided the bulk of the army and economic activity. How could a state demand military service from a class systematically excluded from political power? The historical answer: it couldn't, indefinitely. The Conflict of the Orders (~494–287 BC) is the long resolution.

## Full Exposition

Key moves in the Conflict of the Orders:

- **494 BC — First Secession of the Plebs.** Plebeians withdraw from the city; in response, the office of *tribune of the plebs* is created — magistrates who exist solely to protect plebeian interests, with the power of intercessio (veto) over patrician magistrates' actions.
- **451–450 BC — Twelve Tables.** First written codification of Roman law, removing a key patrician advantage (knowledge of unwritten customary law).
- **445 BC — Lex Canuleia.** Legalizes marriage between patricians and plebeians.
- **367 BC — Licinian-Sextian Laws.** One of the two consuls must be plebeian.
- **300 BC — Lex Ogulnia.** Major priesthoods opened to plebeians.
- **287 BC — Lex Hortensia.** Resolutions of the plebeian assembly (*plebiscita*) become binding on all Romans without Senate ratification. Conventionally treated as the end of the Conflict.

By the late 3rd century BC, the patrician–plebeian distinction is largely formal. A new aristocracy — the *nobilitas* — has formed, comprising the descendants of any family (patrician *or* plebeian) that has held the consulship. The distinction persists in religious and ceremonial roles into the Empire but loses its political bite.

## Analogies

- The English barons forcing concessions from the king (Magna Carta, 1215) — a long aristocratic-vs-popular negotiation that reshapes constitutional structure
- The slow extension of the franchise in 19th-century Britain (Reform Acts)
- Any historical process of incorporating a previously-excluded group into the political class

## Counter-Examples / Boundaries

- The patrician–plebeian line was *hereditary* — a wealthy plebeian could not become a patrician (with rare exceptions by special grant)
- The Conflict did *not* address slavery, non-citizen status, or the position of women — it was an internal struggle within the male citizen body
- After ~287 BC, the more politically meaningful distinction was the nobilitas / non-nobilitas line, not patrician / plebeian

## Applications

- The *cursus honorum* takes its mature form only after most offices are opened to plebeians
- The tribune of the plebs becomes one of the great destabilizing offices of the Late Republic (Tiberius Gracchus uses it to legislate around the Senate in 133 BC — directly invoking the original plebeian protective function for radical purposes)
- Polybius's mixed-constitution analysis is partly possible only because the Conflict had resolved into a stable mixed system
