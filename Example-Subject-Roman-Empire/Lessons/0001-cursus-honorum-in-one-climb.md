# The cursus honorum in one climb

> This markdown note is the source of truth; a rendered `0001-cursus-honorum-in-one-climb.html` sits alongside (built from the bundled `_teaching-engine/_assets/lesson.css`).

## The one win

By the end you can name the rungs of the Republican *cursus honorum* in order and say what each one **constrained** — the scaffold that every later Roman political story hangs on.

## Teach

The *cursus honorum* was a **legally fixed** career ladder, not a custom: **quaestor → (aedile) → praetor → consul**, with minimum ages and mandatory gaps between offices (Beard, *SPQR*, ch. 4–5). The censorship sat above as a later capstone.

The point isn't the list — it's the **fixedness**. Because you couldn't skip rungs, every ambition had to route *through* the sequence, at prescribed ages. That routing is precisely where late-Republic pressure built: men who couldn't advance fast enough by the rules (Pompey, Caesar) had reasons to bend or break them, and the breaking is the story of the Republic's end (→ [[Cursus-Honorum]]).

## Try it (retrieval)

Without looking back: write the four main offices **in order**, the rough minimum age for consul, and one sentence on why the *fixedness* — not the offices themselves — is what made the ladder politically load-bearing. Then check yourself against [[Cursus-Honorum]].
