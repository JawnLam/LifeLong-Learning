# Founding of Rome (753 BC, traditional)

## What Happened

According to Roman tradition, Romulus founded the city of Rome on April 21, 753 BC, after killing his twin brother Remus in a dispute over which of the seven hills the new city should occupy. Romulus became the first of seven kings. The traditional date and narrative are preserved most fully in [[Livy]] Book 1 and Plutarch's *Life of Romulus*.

Modern archaeology shows continuous settlement on the Palatine and other hills from the 9th century BC onward; what the Romans called "founding" was probably a process of synoecism — the gradual coalescence of hilltop villages into a single urban community — that happened over generations during the 8th and 7th centuries BC.

## Why It Mattered

- **As legend:** The founding myth shapes Roman self-understanding for the next thousand years. Augustus consciously evokes Romulus when remaking Rome; the term *Romulus* recurs as a Roman archetype of city-founder authority.
- **As history:** The actual urbanization of the site — wherever exactly it falls — is the beginning of the city that will become the western Mediterranean's dominant power.
- **As trope:** "Founded by a fratricide" is read by some ancient and modern commentators as a dark structural omen — a city whose origin was already violent.

## Causes

- **Geographic:** The Tiber bend at the seven hills provides a defensible position, a river crossing, and access to salt routes
- **Demographic:** Iron Age settlement pressure in central Italy; Latin-speaking peoples consolidating
- **Cultural:** The mythic narrative is later — possibly partly Greek-influenced (Aeneas / Trojan-origin overlay added in the 3rd–2nd century BC)

## Sources / Disputes

- **Livy Book 1** — canonical narrative; Romulus, Remus, Lupercal, the rape of the Sabines, etc.
- **Plutarch, *Life of Romulus*** — Greek-perspective biographical treatment
- **Virgil, *Aeneid*** — links the Roman foundation to the Trojan refugee Aeneas; written under Augustus
- **Modern archaeology** — Andrea Carandini's excavations (controversially) date a wall on the Palatine to the mid-8th century BC, claiming to corroborate the traditional founding date. Most archaeologists treat this with caution.

The disagreement between literary tradition and archaeology is a useful first lesson in *how Romans wrote about their own origins*: the date 753 BC is precise to a fault, the narrative is dramatically structured, and the historical reality is messier and slower.
