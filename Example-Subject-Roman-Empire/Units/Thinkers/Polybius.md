# Polybius

## Sketch

Greek statesman from Megalopolis (Achaean League) who was taken to Rome as a political hostage in 167 BC after Rome's victory at Pydna. He spent decades among the Roman elite — most famously as a member of the household of Scipio Aemilianus, the man who would destroy Carthage in 146 BC. From this insider vantage Polybius wrote the *Histories*, a 40-book account of Rome's rise from a regional Italian power to ruler of the Mediterranean (264–146 BC).

He is the first major historian to take seriously the question: *what is it about Roman institutions that produced this result?* — and his answer, in Book 6, is the foundational text of mixed-constitution political theory.

## Method / Stance

- **Pragmatic / political-analytical history**, in conscious contrast to dramatic or moralizing history. Polybius wanted history to be *useful* for statesmen.
- **Insider vantage.** He observed the Roman elite at close range and traveled with Scipio Aemilianus on campaign; this gives weight to his institutional descriptions.
- **Greek theoretical framework.** He inherits Aristotle's constitutional taxonomy and Greek cyclical-history theory (anacyclosis — the natural cycle of constitutional forms). The mixed-constitution thesis arises from applying this framework to Rome.
- **Pro-Roman but not uncritical.** Polybius is broadly admiring but capable of analytical distance.
- **Reliability:** Generally high for events he witnessed or could verify from elite informants. Less reliable for earlier Roman history (which he gets second-hand).

## Key Works

- **Histories** — the master work. 40 books originally; Books 1–5 survive intact (covering 264–216 BC), with substantial fragments of Book 6 (the constitutional analysis), Books 7–15 (Hannibalic War aftermath, Roman expansion east), and scattered later books.
  - **Book 1** — First Punic War
  - **Book 6** — the mixed-constitution analysis. The single most important piece for a generalist study.
  - **Book 38** — fall of Carthage (146 BC), which Polybius witnessed in person
