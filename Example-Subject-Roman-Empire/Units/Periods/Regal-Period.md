# Regal Period (753 – 509 BC, traditional)

## Character

The semi-legendary period in which Rome was a kingdom ruled by seven kings, according to the traditional Roman account preserved by Livy and others. Modern historians treat the period skeptically — the seven-king sequence is suspiciously neat, the dates are too round, and much of the narrative reads as later retrojection. But something happened in this period: Rome grew from a hilltop settlement on the Tiber into a sizable city with civic institutions, religious practice, and the territorial base for what came next.

## Key Institutions in Force

- **Monarchy** — the *rex*, with religious, military, and judicial functions
- **Senate** (early form) — advisory council of clan elders (the *patres*)
- **Curiate Assembly** (*comitia curiata*) — early popular assembly organized by curiae
- **Patrician–plebeian distinction** in some early form (though the canonical institutional shape develops in the early Republic)

## Major Events Within

- [[Founding-Of-Rome]] (753 BC, traditional date; Romulus and Remus)
- The reigns of the seven kings (Romulus, Numa Pompilius, Tullus Hostilius, Ancus Marcius, Tarquinius Priscus, Servius Tullius, Tarquinius Superbus)
- Servian Reforms — attributed to Servius Tullius; reorganized the citizen body into the centuriate assembly (*comitia centuriata*) based on wealth and military equipment
- [[Expulsion-Of-Tarquins]] (509 BC, traditional date) — Lucius Tarquinius Superbus, the seventh king, deposed; the Republic begins

## Inflection Out

The expulsion of Tarquinius Superbus in 509 BC. According to the tradition (Livy Book 1), the king's son raped Lucretia, a noblewoman; her suicide triggered an aristocratic revolt led by Lucius Junius Brutus. The monarchy was abolished and replaced with annual elected magistrates (the early Republic). Modern historians accept the broad outline — a transition from monarchy to oligarchic Republic around the late 6th century BC — while treating the Lucretia story as foundational legend rather than fact.

## Concepts Operative in This Period

- The seeds of [[Mos-Maiorum]] — the unwritten customs that will define Republican Rome largely formed in this period
- Early forms of citizen assembly that will mature into Republican institutions
- The patrician *gens* (clan) structure that underpins the early Republican aristocracy

## Thinkers / Sources for This Period

- [[Livy]] — Book 1, the canonical narrative. Read for *what Romans believed about their own origins*, not for what reliably happened.
- Dionysius of Halicarnassus, *Roman Antiquities* — Greek perspective on the same legendary material
- Modern: Cornell, *The Beginnings of Rome* (academic counterweight)
