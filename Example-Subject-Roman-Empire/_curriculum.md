# Curriculum — The Rise and Fall of the Roman Empire

> **The six-phase roadmap. Source order, exit criteria, hours. Designed for a generalist mastery study at 3–5 hours per week over 12–18 months.**

## Study constants

- **Total estimated hours:** 200–250 core, +50 hours optional deep-dives
- **Delivery mode:** Primary sources in translation + accessible modern secondaries, Socratic dialogue with AI assistant
- **Math stance:** Minimal (some demographic/economic numeracy late in study)
- **Synthesis cadence:** Weekly journals, monthly essays, phase-end translations, quarterly drafts per `_teaching-engine/04-SYNTHESIS-CADENCE.md`

## Phase overview

| # | Phase | Span (BC/AD) | Core hrs | Deep-dive hrs |
|---|-------|--------------|---------:|--------------:|
| 1 | Foundations: Monarchy through Middle Republic | 753 BC – 146 BC | 30–35 | +10 |
| 2 | Late Republic and the Augustan Revolution | 146 BC – 14 AD | 35–40 | +10 |
| 3 | The Principate at Height | 14 AD – 180 AD | 30–35 | +10 |
| 4 | Crisis and Recovery | 180 AD – 337 AD | 30–35 | +10 |
| 5 | Late Antiquity and the Fall of the West | 337 AD – 476 AD | 35–40 | +10 |
| 6 | Continuation, Aftermath, and the Question of Why | 476 AD – present | 40–50 | +10 |

Phase 6 is heavier because it includes the major synthesis work — Gibbon, modern historiographical comparison, and the user's own structural arc essay.

## Phase 1 — Foundations: Monarchy through Middle Republic (753 BC – 146 BC)

**Core question:** What kind of political community was Rome before it had an empire, and how did its institutions equip it to acquire one?

### Source order

1. Mary Beard, *SPQR: A History of Ancient Rome* (2015) — chapters 1–4 (origins through mid-Republic). Modern secondary, accessible orientation.
2. Livy, *Ab Urbe Condita* — Books 1–5 (founding, regal period, early Republic, Gallic sack). Primary source in translation; read selectively for narrative flavor and Roman self-understanding.
3. Polybius, *Histories* — Book 6 (the mixed-constitution analysis). Primary source; the canonical theoretical account of Rome's institutions by a near-contemporary observer.

### Optional deep dive

- Plutarch, selected early *Lives* (Romulus, Numa, Coriolanus, Camillus) — for the legendary tradition
- Cornell, *The Beginnings of Rome* (1995) — for an academic counterweight to Livy's mythologizing

### Exit criteria

- [ ] Distinguish the regal period, early Republic, and middle Republic by their institutional character and load-bearing events
- [ ] Explain the cursus honorum and how it shaped Roman political behavior
- [ ] Explain Polybius's mixed-constitution thesis and assess whether it accurately describes mid-Republic Rome
- [ ] Narrate the three Punic Wars at a high level — who fought, what changed, why the third was different from the first two
- [ ] Place ten Phase 1 events on a timeline without notes

### Phase 1 Units

- **Concepts:** Mos-Maiorum, Cursus-Honorum, Mixed-Constitution, Imperium, Patrician-Plebeian-Distinction
- **Thinkers:** Polybius, Livy
- **Periods:** Regal-Period, Early-Republic, Middle-Republic
- **Events:** Founding-Of-Rome, Expulsion-Of-Tarquins, Sack-Of-Rome-By-Gauls, Second-Punic-War, Destruction-Of-Carthage

## Phase 2 — Late Republic and the Augustan Revolution (146 BC – 14 AD)

**Core question:** How did a political system that worked for 350 years break down in 100 years, and what did Augustus build in its place?

### Source order

1. Beard, *SPQR* — chapters 5–8 (Gracchi through Augustus)
2. Tom Holland, *Rubicon: The Last Years of the Roman Republic* (2003) — accessible narrative of the breakdown
3. Plutarch, *Lives* — selected (Tiberius Gracchus, Gaius Gracchus, Marius, Sulla, Pompey, Caesar, Cicero, Cato the Younger, Antony) — primary-tradition biographies
4. Suetonius, *The Twelve Caesars* — Julius and Augustus (the first two)
5. Augustus, *Res Gestae Divi Augusti* — short, essential; Augustus's own account of what he did

### Optional deep dive

- Sallust, *War with Catiline* and *War with Jugurtha* — primary-source narratives of late-Republic dysfunction
- Cicero, selected letters and speeches — the voice of the Republic as it dies

### Exit criteria

- [ ] Identify the structural pressures that destabilized the Republic (Gracchan reforms, professional armies, generalissimo politics, the Italian question)
- [ ] Name and place: the Gracchi, Marius, Sulla, the First Triumvirate, the Second Triumvirate, the civil wars
- [ ] Explain what Augustus actually changed institutionally — and what he preserved as legal fiction
- [ ] Articulate the Principate as a constitutional innovation: explain why historians call it a "veiled monarchy"

### Phase 2 Units

- **Concepts:** Patron-Client-System, Tribune-Of-The-Plebs, Proscription, Triumvirate, Principate, Imperator
- **Thinkers:** Cicero, Sallust, Suetonius, Augustus (as primary source)
- **Periods:** Late-Republic, Augustan-Revolution
- **Events:** Tribunate-Of-Tiberius-Gracchus, Marian-Reforms, Sulla's-Dictatorship, Crossing-The-Rubicon, Ides-Of-March, Battle-Of-Actium, Augustan-Settlement

## Phase 3 — The Principate at Height (14 AD – 180 AD)

**Core question:** What did the Roman world look like during the period traditionally called "happy" — and what were the structural weaknesses Edward Gibbon and others would later read as the seeds of decline?

### Source order

1. Beard, *SPQR* — chapters 9–12 (Principate through Antonines)
2. Tacitus, *Annals* — selected (Tiberius through Nero); the canonical critical voice on the early imperial period
3. Tacitus, *Histories* — Year of the Four Emperors (69 AD)
4. Suetonius, *The Twelve Caesars* — Tiberius through Domitian
5. Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* — for the Antonine voice at its apex
6. Edward Gibbon, *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire* (1776–88) — chapters 1–3 (the famous "age of the Antonines" opening)

### Optional deep dive

- Adrian Goldsworthy, *Pax: War and Peace in Rome's Golden Age* (2023) — modern counterweight to the romanticized "Pax Romana"
- Pliny the Younger, *Letters* — daily life in the imperial period

### Exit criteria

- [ ] Distinguish the Julio-Claudian, Flavian, and Antonine dynasties by character and load-bearing emperors
- [ ] Explain the Year of the Four Emperors (69 AD) and what it revealed about the Principate
- [ ] Articulate the "Five Good Emperors" thesis and assess what was actually good about it (the adoption succession) and what was contingent
- [ ] Read Tacitus on Tiberius or Nero and place his rhetorical posture (hostile, retrospective, senatorial)

### Phase 3 Units

- **Concepts:** Julio-Claudian-Dynasty, Adoption-Succession, Limes (frontiers), Damnatio-Memoriae, Imperial-Cult, Praetorian-Guard
- **Thinkers:** Tacitus, Marcus Aurelius (as primary source)
- **Periods:** Julio-Claudian-Period, Year-Of-Four-Emperors, Flavian-Period, Antonine-Period
- **Events:** Great-Fire-Of-Rome (64 AD), Eruption-Of-Vesuvius (79 AD), Death-Of-Domitian (96 AD), Death-Of-Marcus-Aurelius (180 AD)

## Phase 4 — Crisis and Recovery (180 AD – 337 AD)

**Core question:** Rome nearly broke in the third century. Why didn't it? What did Diocletian and Constantine rebuild that prevented collapse — and what did they change forever?

### Source order

1. Beard, *SPQR* — chapter 13 (the third-century crisis)
2. Cassius Dio, *Roman History* — Severan books, surviving sections
3. Historia Augusta — selected (despite its unreliability, the canonical Latin source for the third-century emperors); read with critical apparatus
4. Diocletian, *Edict on Maximum Prices* (301 AD) — short primary source; institutional flavor of the Dominate
5. Constantine, edicts on the church and on currency — primary sources for the imperial-Christian turn
6. David Potter, *The Roman Empire at Bay, AD 180–395* (2nd ed., 2014) — modern academic synthesis

### Optional deep dive

- Peter Brown, *The World of Late Antiquity, AD 150–750* (1971) — opens Phase 5's interpretive frame early

### Exit criteria

- [ ] Explain why the third-century crisis nearly destroyed Rome (military emperors, hyperinflation, plague, barbarian pressure, internal succession failure)
- [ ] Distinguish the Dominate from the Principate institutionally — what changed in how emperors ruled and were seen
- [ ] Explain the Tetrarchy and why it failed even though Diocletian designed it
- [ ] Articulate the significance of Constantine's conversion (313 Edict of Milan, 325 Council of Nicaea) for both Rome and Christianity

### Phase 4 Units

- **Concepts:** Severan-Dynasty, Soldier-Emperors, Tetrarchy, Dominate, Edict-Of-Milan, Council-Of-Nicaea, Solidus (new gold coinage)
- **Thinkers:** Cassius-Dio, Eusebius (Christian historiography enters)
- **Periods:** Severan-Period, Crisis-Of-The-Third-Century, Diocletianic-Reform, Constantinian-Period
- **Events:** Assassination-Of-Commodus (192 AD), Year-Of-Five-Emperors (193), Battle-Of-Edessa (260, capture of Valerian), Accession-Of-Diocletian (284), Battle-Of-Milvian-Bridge (312), Foundation-Of-Constantinople (330)

## Phase 5 — Late Antiquity and the Fall of the West (337 AD – 476 AD)

**Core question:** What actually happened when "Rome fell"? Was it fall, transformation, or something in between — and why is the answer so contested?

### Source order

1. Beard, *SPQR* — chapter 14 / epilogue (limited coverage; Beard mostly stops at Caracalla)
2. Ammianus Marcellinus, *Res Gestae* — the last great Roman historian, covering 353–378; eyewitness to Adrianople
3. Augustine, *City of God* — selected books (esp. I, V, XIX); the canonical Christian response to the sack of Rome (410)
4. Peter Heather, *The Fall of the Roman Empire: A New History of Rome and the Barbarians* (2005) — the "barbarian pressure caused the fall" thesis
5. Bryan Ward-Perkins, *The Fall of Rome and the End of Civilization* (2005) — the "things really did fall apart" thesis
6. Peter Brown, *The Rise of Western Christendom* (2003) — the "transformation, not collapse" thesis

### Optional deep dive

- Procopius, *Wars* (especially the Vandalic and Gothic wars) — for the Eastern continuation reasserting itself
- Salvian, *On the Government of God* — a fifth-century Christian voice on Roman moral decay

### Exit criteria

- [ ] Narrate the fifth century: Theodosian division (395), Visigothic sack of Rome (410), Vandal sack (455), deposition of Romulus Augustulus (476)
- [ ] Explain the Battle of Adrianople (378) and why historians consider it pivotal
- [ ] Articulate Heather's, Ward-Perkins's, and Brown's interpretations of "the fall" — what each emphasizes, what each downplays
- [ ] Read a passage of Augustine's *City of God* and place his theological response to 410 in its political context

### Phase 5 Units

- **Concepts:** Foederati (federated barbarians), Theodosian-Division, Sack-Of-Rome-Trope, Barbarian-Kingdoms, Roman-Christian-Synthesis
- **Thinkers:** Ammianus-Marcellinus, Augustine, Peter-Heather, Bryan-Ward-Perkins, Peter-Brown
- **Periods:** Constantinian-Aftermath, Theodosian-Period, End-Of-Western-Empire
- **Events:** Battle-Of-Adrianople (378), Theodosian-Division (395), Sack-Of-Rome-By-Alaric (410), Sack-Of-Rome-By-Vandals (455), Deposition-Of-Romulus-Augustulus (476)

## Phase 6 — Continuation, Aftermath, and the Question of Why (476 AD – present)

**Core question:** Rome didn't fully end in 476. What continued? And across 1,500 years of subsequent historiography, what do we now think we know about *why* it fell?

This phase carries the integrative synthesis weight of the entire study.

### Source order

1. Edward Gibbon, *Decline and Fall* — selected chapters (esp. ch. 38 "General Observations on the Fall of the Roman Empire in the West"). The canonical text of the decline thesis.
2. Survey of major decline theses (covered via secondary literature):
   - Gibbon's Christianity / barbarism thesis (1776–88)
   - Pirenne's Islam-driven economic-disconnect thesis (*Mohammed and Charlemagne*, 1937)
   - Walter Goffart's accommodation thesis (1980)
   - Heather / Ward-Perkins position (2005, completed in Phase 5)
   - Brown's late-antique transformation thesis (1971+)
3. Survey of the Byzantine continuation (light pass):
   - Justinian's reconquest attempts (530s–550s)
   - The 1453 fall of Constantinople as the "real" end
4. Mary Beard, *Twelve Caesars: Images of Power* (2021) — Rome in Western imagination, as a closing meditation
5. The user's own integrative synthesis (phase deliverable)

### Major synthesis work

The weight of this phase is producing the user's own structural arc essay (10,000–15,000 words). This serves as the master Phase-End Translation for the whole study.

### Exit criteria

- [ ] Articulate at least three coherent theses for why Rome fell, with reasoning for which the user finds most persuasive
- [ ] Explain what continued in the East — and what *didn't* continue in the West — with reference to institutions, economy, and culture
- [ ] Produce a finished long-form synthesis on the structural arc of Rome, stand-alone, defensible to a follow-up reader who pushes back
- [ ] Be able to deliver the 30-minute "structural arc of Rome" conversation that was the original goal in `_subject.md`

### Phase 6 Units

- **Concepts:** Decline-Thesis, Late-Antique-Transformation-Thesis, Roman-Legacy, Byzantine-Continuation
- **Thinkers:** Edward-Gibbon, Henri-Pirenne, Walter-Goffart (additions to the modern-historian roster begun in Phase 5)
- **Periods:** Byzantine-Continuation, Western-Successor-Kingdoms
- **Events:** Justinian's-Reconquest, Sack-Of-Constantinople (1204), Fall-Of-Constantinople (1453)
