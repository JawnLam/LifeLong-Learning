# Livy (Titus Livius)

## Sketch

Roman historian from Patavium (Padua) who lived through the entire arc from the Late Republic into the Augustan Principate. His monumental *Ab Urbe Condita* — "From the Founding of the City" — set out to tell the whole story of Rome from 753 BC to his own day in 142 books. Thirty-five survive: Books 1–10 (founding through 293 BC) and 21–45 (Second Punic War through 167 BC). The lost books are summarized in ancient *Periochae* (book-by-book outlines) that survive separately.

Livy wrote under Augustus, in a Rome that was self-consciously rebuilding its civic identity after a century of civil war. His project was partly historical, partly moral: to recover and celebrate the virtues that had made Rome great, and to invite Romans to see their own age in light of that earlier example.

## Method / Stance

- **Dramatic / moralizing history**, in conscious distinction from Polybius's pragmatic-analytical approach. Livy wants you to feel the Roman virtues, not just analyze them.
- **Patriotic frame.** Livy is sympathetic to Rome and to the Roman aristocratic tradition. Critical readers (ancient and modern) note his idealization of early Rome.
- **Source reliability varies sharply by period.** For events within memory and based on documentary sources (mid-Republic forward, after ~300 BC), Livy is reasonably reliable. For early Rome (regal period, early Republic) he is dramatizing legend with limited critical apparatus.
- **Augustan-era voice.** Written in an age that was both rebuilding and rewriting Roman identity. The retrojections of contemporary concerns into early Roman material are part of the source.
- **Reliability:** Use with the period in mind. Books 1–5 (founding through Gallic sack) are legend dressed as history; Books 21–30 (Second Punic War) are real history with literary flourishes; Books 31–45 are the closest Livy gets to documentary history.

## Key Works

- **Ab Urbe Condita** — the great project
  - **Books 1–5** — founding through the Gallic sack (753–390 BC). The "legendary" history.
  - **Books 6–10** — early Republic through 293 BC. Conflict of the Orders, Samnite Wars.
  - **Books 11–20** — lost. Covered ~292–219 BC (mid-Republic consolidation).
  - **Books 21–30** — Second Punic War (218–201 BC). One of the great historical narratives in any language.
  - **Books 31–45** — Roman expansion east, 200–167 BC.
  - **Books 46–142** — lost. Covered 167 BC down to ~9 BC (Livy's own time).
