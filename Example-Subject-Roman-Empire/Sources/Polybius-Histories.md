# Polybius, *Histories* — Book 6

## What this source is

Book 6 of Polybius's *Histories* — the great theoretical excursus on the Roman constitution, embedded in his narrative of Rome's rise to Mediterranean dominance. Book 6 survives substantially in the original Greek, with some passages lost. The Oxford World's Classics translation (Waterfield) is the most accessible modern English version; for serious work, pair with the Loeb (Paton) for facing-page Greek.

This is one of the great primary-source texts of political theory, period — read by everyone from Cicero to Machiavelli to Montesquieu to Madison.

## Why it matters here

For Phase 1, Polybius Book 6 provides:

- The mixed-constitution thesis ([[Mixed-Constitution]]) in its founding formulation
- A near-contemporary institutional description of mid-Republic Rome
- The analytical frame that all later Roman political theory builds on
- An early example of comparative constitutional thought (Polybius compares Rome to Sparta and Carthage)

The other surviving books of the *Histories* (1–5 in full, plus fragments of others) are excellent for the narrative of the Punic Wars, but Phase 1 uses primarily Book 6 for the institutional analysis. Other books come back later (especially for [[Second-Punic-War]] in deeper sessions).

## Approach notes

- **Pace:** Book 6 is short (a long evening's reading). Read once for the structure of the argument, then re-read specific sections when working on related Units.
- **Depth:** This is the densest piece of theory in the Phase 1 reading. Take notes on Polybius's specific claims about *which* institution corresponds to *which* constitutional element (monarchy/aristocracy/democracy) — these claims are the load-bearing content for [[Mixed-Constitution]].
- **What to watch for:** Where does Polybius's Greek theoretical framework strain against the Roman reality? Where does his framework do real work that Roman self-description couldn't do?
- **Pairing:** Read after Beard's relevant chapter (for context on Polybius's career and biases); pair with the [[Polybius]] thinker Unit.
