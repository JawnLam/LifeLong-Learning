# Expulsion of the Tarquins (509 BC, traditional)

## What Happened

According to the Roman tradition (Livy Book 1; Dionysius of Halicarnassus), the last king of Rome, Lucius Tarquinius Superbus, was driven out of the city after his son Sextus Tarquinius raped Lucretia, the wife of a Roman nobleman. Lucretia's suicide — public, in front of her husband and Lucius Junius Brutus — triggered an aristocratic revolt. Brutus led the rising. The monarchy was abolished. In its place: two annually-elected consuls (originally called *praetors*), drawn from the patrician aristocracy. The Republic begins.

Modern historians accept the broad outline — a transition from kingship to oligarchic republic in roughly the late 6th century BC — while treating the Lucretia narrative as dramatized political legend rather than reliable history.

## Why It Mattered

- **Constitutional revolution:** Replaces sole monarchical authority with two annual collegial magistrates. Sets the template — collegiality, term limits, annual rotation — that defines the Republican magistracy for the next 450 years.
- **Founding myth of the Republic:** Just as Romulus founds the city, Brutus founds the Republic. The Roman aristocracy will reach back to this moment for legitimacy whenever the Republic is threatened.
- **Anti-monarchical taboo:** The Romans of the Republic carry a deep, almost religious aversion to *regnum* — to anyone who looks like they might be trying to be king. This taboo will be a weapon used against Caesar four and a half centuries later. (When the conspirators assassinate Caesar in 44 BC, Marcus Junius Brutus — one of the assassins — consciously evokes his ancestor Lucius Junius Brutus, the king-expeller.)

## Causes

- **Local political dynamics:** Tarquinius Superbus, the seventh and last king, was tyrannical even by ancient standards in the tradition (executions of senators, exclusion of the elite from rule). Aristocratic resentment is plausibly the real underlying cause.
- **Etruscan retreat:** The Tarquins are traditionally Etruscan-origin (or Etruscan-influenced). Their fall coincides with a broader weakening of Etruscan power in central Italy.
- **The Lucretia narrative:** Almost certainly a literary-political dramatization, but it functions in the tradition as the spark.

## Sources / Disputes

- **Livy Book 1, chapters 56–60** — the canonical narrative
- **Dionysius of Halicarnassus, *Roman Antiquities* Book 4** — parallel Greek account
- **Modern reconstructions** generally accept (a) that the kingship ended around 509 BC, give or take a few decades, and (b) that the transition was contested and not purely constitutional, but reject the specific Lucretia narrative as foundational legend

A real question: was the transition as clean and as early as the tradition makes it? Some historians argue for a more gradual transition extending into the 5th century, with the consulship as a name applied retrospectively to magistrates who looked different from later consuls.
